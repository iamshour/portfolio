Project

Carsam
Car Rental Booking app

Genre

UI/UX

Year

2020

Org

Side Project

Situation

I started this project as part of a UI/UX professional design certification program offered by Coursera. (See Certificate here). It entitles students to design two simple UI screen mockups around cars & tranportation using Figma. However, I took decided to take it to a whole new level, and turned it into to a fully designed mobile application, that i called “Carsam”, out of pure intuition. It helps users to find a convinient car for rental.

Preparation

Sketching, Color Palette, Font, & Tools used

Started this project sketching simple mockups and very general wireframes that i can use and rely on as references while designing the actual app UI.
After concluding a basic look and feel of the app’s UI, it was time for picking the right color Palette to work with. Eventually, I decided to pick red as the primary color, with two darker shades, relying on its effect on grabbing users’ attention and its emotional effect in general.
For instance, in order to maintain any users’ interest in any app, website, platform, blog, etc, a good designer would better control this psycholgical factor by choosing a relatively easening color, such as red, or picking a general tone around the design interface, that a user might find cool and special to stick around more.
Next, my choice for the primary font was Google’s popular “poppins” font , for its smoothness and simplicity, and the fact that its an increasingly popular font around the web recently.
In addition to using a selection of Iconify & flaticon’s pretty cool icons throughout my project

Action

My Goal is to essetially turn sketches/ideas into engaging user interface, using previously mentioned design resources and tools.
Steps are illustrated below:

Sign in / Sign Up Interface

Chose an alternatively dark Login interface, based on the popularity of the general “dark theme” around the web and mobile apps lately. Decided on including three sign in/up methods:

  1. Through Google account
  2. Through Facebook, both which users find as the easiest ways for hooking up with apps nowadays
  3. Through a user’s phone number, by which an sms would be sent to users including a code

Forward Navigation UI

Throughout this section, I’ll elaborate on each design UI based on levels of Forward navigation. To illustrate, General navigation throughout the app is primarly considered as Forward navigation, which is referred by Google’s material design to moving between screens at consecutive levels of hierarchy, steps in a flow, or across an app. I adopted its embedding behavior into containers (i.e. cards) buttons to proceed, or by using search.

a. First Level Navigation

After deciding on five popular car-categories: Sports cars, Sedan Cars, SUVs, Muscle cars, and Vintage cars, the original plan was to design a drop-down menu to let users choose a chosen rental car. However, after trying the drop-down interface, it didn’t look as an effective yet good looking option. So i decided on a slider element, located at the top center side. The default option is “Sedan cars” category, as it is generally more popular and used among others, where users will have the ability to switch right - or left -to navigate between categories.
Organized each available car option in a simple card, containing:

  • Picture of the car itself on the left side
  • Further details about it on the right side, such as its full name, it’s brand, model year
  • Features of each car (such as transmission, Parking, Wifi availibilty, snowflake wheels variancy), aligned horizontally at the bottom of the right side, presented as icons
  • added a drop shadow
  • presented cards vertically so that users can swipe up/down for navigation

Added search & sort features through their icons aligning both on the top right side of the viewport. Both are essential to improve user experience, where it makes finding any specific rental car easier and faster.
Pressing on the sort icon will display an overlay box, designed as follows:

  • Width & Height: Almost half of the viewport’s area
  • aligned in the center of the screen
  • 16px border-radius around all corners
  • a scrim placed directly below the overlay box and can be tapped or clicked to dismiss the box
  • Offered users two options: the first is sorting cards by alphabetical order, ascending or descending, and the second is sorting cards by rental fees, from the most expensive to the least, and vice versa.

Designed a “Rate us” card, that pops up after a user completes a rental activity, displayed in the middle of the screen, and designed it as follows:

  • Width & Height: Almost half of the viewport’s area
  • 16px border-radius around all corners
  • a scrim placed directly below the card and can be tapped or clicked to dismiss the card


Introduced a cool animation effect: while pressing on one of the emojis, a bigger colored emoji pops up, then after releasing the press, all of the contents dissappear and gets replaced with another info notes:

  • In case of pressing on the happy emoji, a Thank You note is displayed
  • In case of pressing on the sad emoji, an apologizing note is displayed along asking the user to contact us for further details.

b. Second Level Navigation

Second Level of navigation in this app is when users choose a car from the list, and decide to read more info about it, which would help in making up their mind for a final decision (next step). This screen UI is designed as follows:

  • Car’s name beside the backwords icon, on the top left side
  • An option to add the car to “Favorites” page, with it’s icon aligned on the top right side
  • Each car’s main picture in the center on the viewport, with a pagination icon right underneath it
  • Overview section, which adds more details about each car
  • "Continue" botton aligned in the bottom center, to proceed with the operation to the next step

c. Third Level Navigation

Third and Last step in Rental operation is after a user makes up his mind and choose a car to rent.
Users have the ability to:

  • Choose pickup/Return dates and time
  • Overall fees are thus displayed
  • Choose a payment method, whether it’s Cash or using a credit card
  • Confirm operation by pressing on the “confirm” button

Navigation Drawer

Designed a dismissible navigation drawer, which can be opened by tapping the navigation menu icon in the top left side of the app, and closed by tapping the close icon in the top right side of the drawer. It opens from the left side of the screen, and is elevated above all app elements when active.
Designed it’s content as follows:

  • A slightly blurred background containing a sports car parked on the right side of the screen
  • Content of the drawer is aligned vertically in the center of the drawer
  • Content organized in chronological order of importance
  • Logo of the app is placed centerally underneath the content

Notifications

Followed the same cards pattern, by which every specific notification is shaped inside a card, and each one contains a picture of the related car aligned on the left, and its content aligned on the right. Each card is also given a drop shadow, with a lighter shade of red as the background color (Hex #C08383).
The four general categories users would be notified about:

  • When someone views a car which is in a user’s favorites list
  • When a new car has been added
  • When someone likes/comments on another user’s review
  • when someone rates a car in a user’s favorites list

Favorites

This page holds all cars that a user have added after hitting the heart shaped icon in the main interface. Very similar to Notifications UI, each card holds a picture of each liked car on the left side of the card, and content on the right side, with the same drop shadow and background color used in notifications UI.

Reviews

A classic interface that users would feel comfortable and familiar with. Users can add a review, rate a specifi car, or even like/comment on other users reviews. They can also hide a specific reviewby simply pressing on the close icon aligned on the right side of each review section.

Final Thoughts

As this project was my first real entrance into the world of UI/UX design, it was a fruitful period which I applied my skills and design inspiration into a full engaging devliverable. Although it wasn’t required to go through any research or survey, I rellied on my knowledge of the car industry in general to support and inspire my last brief skteches and wireframes, which i eventually worked on for about two weeks, more or less, to turn into an interactive mobile interface.
